01The findings
  • Three scenarios map out the possibilities: stay where she is, move home, or return to assisted living after trying home.
  • Over three years, the three scenarios come out similar in cost, within about $10,000 of each other.
  • Whichever scenario, her roughly $90,000 cushion runs dry around Nov 2027 – Feb 2028, and selling the house looks like the only way to keep paying for care after that.
  • If assisted-living costs rise about 5% a year while the house does not, time may favor moving home.
  • Depending on the care she needs at home, moving home ranges from cost-competitive with assisted living to the cheapest of the three by far.
  • The major factor is the home care plan: how much help she needs, what it costs, and whether it can be reliably staffed without falling back on family to cover gaps when staff miss appointments.

03The real question: care at home
How much care does she need at home, what does it cost, and can it be staffed?

At home, both the cost and the safety come down to the care plan:

  • The hours: how many a day she actually needs, and at what skill level.
  • The staffing: whether it can be reliably covered, including backup when someone cannot show up.
  • The insulin (the biggest swing): twice-daily injections are skilled care, not something to put on family. A paid skilled aide (about $50/hour) makes home cost about the same as assisted living; if it can be simplified or self-administered, the rest is mostly personal care (about $30/hour) and home is the cheapest of the three by far.
  • Do this first: a concrete care plan, real hours, who provides them, what they cost, and backup coverage. Resolve it and the financial choice mostly answers itself.
Tangible ways to make the insulin safe at home

Most of the worry about home is really one worry: the insulin. Here is concrete equipment that addresses it, with real prices. At 100 she is on Medicare, which covers most of it.

ToolWhat it doesEst. cost / month
Continuous glucose monitorFreeStyle Libre 3 or Dexcom G7 Reads her sugar all day and alarms on a low, the safety net against taking too much. Family can watch the numbers from a phone. $0–50
Prefilled insulin penwith a simplified once-daily dose Removes the measuring that caused the over-injecting. Dial the units and inject, no syringe to read. ~$35
Smart pill dispenserHero or MedMinder Locks her other medicines, releases the right dose at the right time, and texts family if one is missed. $30–50
The whole safety layer Monitoring, simplified insulin, and managed pills, together. ~$65–135

One-time setup runs about $0 to $200: the monitor reads on a phone she already has, and the dispenser is a subscription. Buying a dispenser outright instead is about $1,000 to $2,000. Medicare covers the monitor and caps the insulin, so the dispenser is the main out-of-pocket piece.

What this swaps out. The costly part of home care was a skilled, insulin-trained aide, about $50/hour, twice a day, coming mainly to give the insulin. This kit can replace that skilled visit. The dose is one she gives herself, or one a regular aide who is already there for showers simply watches her give, with the monitor as the backstop. That trims roughly $1,500 to $3,000 a month off the home plan.

What stays. She still needs hands-on help with bathing, dressing, moving safely, and meals. That does not change, and it is the same in any plan that keeps her at home.

The one hinge. It works if she can press the plunger on a pre-set pen with someone watching. If she truly cannot, the insulin goes back to family or a nurse, so a doctor confirming a simplified once-daily routine is step one.

Why it matters. Cutting that skilled-nursing line is what keeps the monthly burn low enough to leave the house unsold. In the timeline below, the savings otherwise run out around late 2027. A setup like this, if it works for her, could push that out by years, or take selling off the table. Realistically, it is the path that makes moving home possible at all.

05For & against

The same facts weigh differently depending on what the family values most. For each scenario, the case for and the case against.

Scenario A · Stay in assisted living

The case for

  • Skilled help and insulin already handled, around the clock.
  • No move, no repairs to manage right now.

The case against

  • The bill inflates ~5% a year and never stops.
  • It is the opposite of what she has asked for.
Scenario B · Move home

The case for

  • What she wants: her own bed, her own house.
  • Cost-competitive with staying; cheapest if she self-injects.

The case against

  • Safety hinges on the insulin and on caregivers showing up.
  • Upfront repairs and accessibility work come first.
Scenario C · Home, then back

The case for

  • Honors her wish without betting everything on it.
  • Trying and failing costs surprisingly little.

The case against

  • A second move is hard on a centenarian.
  • Needs an assisted-living relationship kept warm in advance.
