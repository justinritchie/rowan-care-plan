What stands out

Three paths, one destination.

Open the model cold and the first surprise is how little the money separates the choices. In every realistic version of events, the house is sold within roughly 16–18 months to keep paying for her care. Across three years the three paths land within about $10,000 of one another.

That reframes the whole decision. This is not primarily a financial question. The dollars come out close enough that they shouldn't be the deciding vote. What differs between the paths is the level of care she can safely receive, the coordination burden on family, and where she spends what may be her last years.

One question moves the numbers more than every other input combined: can she manage her own insulin? If she can manage it, or a trained family member can, living at home costs less than her monthly income, and her savings are preserved. If she needs a paid, medication-certified aide, home costs about four times as much and her cash runs out in little over a year.

The crossover point

When the house has to be sold.

Her liquid savings, about $90,000 in a money market earning ~2%, are the clock. Once they run dry, the house must be sold to keep paying, and that moment arrives at roughly the same time whether she stays or moves. Selling the house is not a feature of one path; it's the shared turning point of nearly all of them.

The variable that decides it

The insulin question decides almost everything.

North Carolina home aides generally cannot give injections, so insulin twice a day means either a medication-certified aide (around $50/hour, with agency visit minimums), or she does it herself. Because she is sharp, self-administration is plausible, and it changes everything downstream.

Everything else in the model (facility inflation, food, monitoring, even the home repairs) is a rounding error next to this one fork. Resolving who handles her insulin is the single most valuable thing the family can do before deciding.

Sensitivity & confidence

How sure are these numbers?

The honest summary: high confidence in the relative picture (the paths cost about the same and the house sells around 16–18 months) and lower confidence in the absolute monthly figures, which ride almost entirely on the care question. Here is where each input sits, and which way it could move things.

InputConfidenceIf it moves…
Facility cost ($6,000) & her income ($1,700)●●●Known figures. Little risk.
Food, monitoring, medical alert●●●Researched, stable. Minor in the total.
Sale-enabling repairs (~$12.6k)●●●Plumbing-to-city is a real-world estimate; could rise. Paid in every path regardless.
Home monthly cost●●●Swings from ~$1,540 to ~$5,900 on care hours. The dominant uncertainty.
House value / sale proceeds (~$232k net)●●●Lower value = the sale funds fewer months; spend-down arrives sooner.
Caregiver hours & rate · insulin self-care●●●The biggest lever. Best case preserves her savings; worst case beats the facility on cost.
Care level if she returns (Scenario C)●●●A step up to skilled/memory care makes the return materially pricier.

How it could shake out, in three strokes. Best: she self-injects, family shares the day, and home runs under her income and the house is never forced to sell. Base: a few hours of paid care daily, and home ≈ facility, house sells in ~15 months. Hard: she needs near-constant or skilled care, so home exceeds the facility, money runs out faster, and the case for staying (or returning) strengthens. Faster facility inflation (7–8%) only widens home's long-run advantage; a lower house value pulls every spend-down date earlier.

For & against · through three sets of eyes

What pulls the decision each way.

The same facts read differently depending on who's weighing them. For each path: the case for, the case against, and how it might land for her, for Dad, and for his brother.

Scenario A

Stay in care

The case for

  • 24/7 staff: the highest level of supervision and the smallest fall-alone risk.
  • Insulin and medications handled by professionals, no family scramble.
  • No repair project under an August deadline; no setup cost or logistics.
  • Built-in social contact and routine.

The case against

  • Highest monthly cost, and it climbs ~5% a year.
  • It is the one thing she has said she does not want.
  • The house is still sold within ~18 months anyway; staying doesn't save it.
  • Loss of home and independence carries its own quiet cost.
GGrandmother

Hardest to accept: it's the opposite of her stated wish.

DDad

Peace of mind on safety, but the guilt of overriding her, and the rising bill.

UHis brother

Lowest day-to-day burden, attractive if he can't take on coordination.

Scenario B

Move home, and it works

The case for

  • It honors what she wants: her own home, her last years.
  • Cheaper per month than the facility, and the gap grows as the facility inflates.
  • Cameras, alert, and delivered meals build a real safety net the family can watch.
  • If she self-injects, it's by far the cheapest path, with savings preserved.

The case against

  • Upfront cost and a repair project racing an Aug 1 date; the plumbing is the risk.
  • Care coordination falls on the family, week after week.
  • A safety gap vs. 24/7 staffing, especially overnight.
  • If her needs rise, it may not hold, which becomes Scenario C.
GGrandmother

This is the answer she's hoping for: dignity, familiarity, home.

DDad

Most rewarding and most demanding; leans yes if the insulin plan is solid.

UHis brother

Supportive if the load is shared and the safety net is genuinely in place.

Scenario C

Home first, then return to care

The case for

  • She gets her wish: months, maybe a year or more, at home.
  • The net extra cost of trying is only ~$6,700, not a financial gamble.
  • The house sale funds the return with ~$140k to spare.
  • You test the preferred option instead of foreclosing it out of caution.

The case against

  • Two moves, disruptive and tiring for a 100-year-old.
  • Some setup money (ramp, alert, moving) isn't recovered.
  • A re-entry deposit, plus the risk a bed isn't available when needed.
  • If she returns needing higher-level care, the second chapter costs more.
GGrandmother

Time at home now, though leaving it a second time would be hard.

DDad

The "try it, with a safety net" middle path, easiest to feel at peace with.

UHis brother

Reasonable if a facility relationship is kept warm in advance.
